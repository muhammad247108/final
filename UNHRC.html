Introduction to the UNHRC

The Human Rights Council is an intergovernmental body made up of 47 States responsible for the promotion and protection of all human rights around the globe. It meets in Geneva and can discuss human rights issues throughout the year. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hrbodies/hrc/home))

Why it matters

It is the UN’s main political forum for human rights. It addresses violations, debates thematic issues, and keeps international attention on accountability and protection. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))

What it does

It reviews human rights situations, holds special sessions for urgent crises, appoints Special Procedures, and runs the Universal Periodic Review of all UN Member States. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Why MUN uses it

The UNHRC committee teaches rights-based diplomacy, evidence-led argumentation, coalition building, and how to balance sovereignty with international responsibility.

Structure of the Council

Membership is elected directly and individually by the General Assembly, seats are distributed across regional groups, and members serve three-year terms with a two-term limit. The Council also has a bureau made of a president and four vice-presidents. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Membership

The Council consists of 47 Member States. Elections take place every year, roughly one-third of the seats are renewed each year, and representation is spread across the five UN regional groups. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

The bureau includes a president and four vice-presidents representing each regional group. In MUN, this helps explain why the chair is so central to order, fairness, and flow. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Main mechanisms and functions

The Council works through standing agenda items, special sessions, the Universal Periodic Review, Special Procedures, and the ability to take action on urgent situations. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))

Universal Periodic Review

A state-led process that reviews the human rights records of all UN Member States and creates a structured cycle of accountability. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Special Procedures

Independent experts or groups who monitor themes and country situations, act as the Council’s eyes and ears, and report on urgent concerns. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Special sessions

The Council can hold special sessions to respond to urgent human rights crises outside its normal calendar. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/about-council))

Rules and procedure

Real UNHRC procedure is centered on sessions, agenda items, resolutions, interactive dialogue, and observer participation. In MUN, those ideas become debate structure, motion handling, and drafting discipline. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))

Real Council procedure

  • The Council meets at least three times a year in Geneva, Switzerland. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))
  • It considers issues through a standing agenda and can also hold special sessions for urgent matters. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))
  • Observers such as NGOs in ECOSOC consultative status may attend and speak in the ways allowed by the Council. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))
  • OHCHR serves as the secretariat for the Council. ([ohchr.org](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/ngo-participation))

What that means in MUN

Delegates should use rights-based reasoning, reference norms and mechanisms, and keep their language formal but persuasive. Strong UNHRC committees reward clarity, credibility, and a realistic understanding of human rights protection.

How UNHRC works in MUN

UNHRC committees are usually policy-heavy and evidence-sensitive. They reward delegates who understand both moral language and practical implementation.

Typical flow of debate

1. Roll call and agenda

The chair opens the session, identifies the topic, and establishes the rights issue or country case to be discussed.

2. Position setting

Delegates explain their state’s approach to human rights, sovereignty, accountability, and cooperation.

3. Alliance building

Members negotiate around shared values, regional priorities, and political sensitivities to build draft texts.

4. Drafting and adoption

The final resolution should include practical remedies, monitoring tools, and implementation pathways.

What strong delegates understand

  • Rights arguments should be backed by mechanisms, not just sentiment.
  • Some states prioritize sovereignty; others emphasize intervention or accountability.
  • Coalitions can form around themes like civil liberties, women’s rights, conflict protection, or institutional reform.
  • Good UNHRC resolutions aim for implementation, monitoring, and measurable outcomes.
  • Language should be diplomatic, balanced, and legally informed.

How to speak like a strong UNHRC delegate

Use a simple logic chain: claim, mechanism, result, impact. That keeps speeches disciplined and persuasive.

Speech formula

  • Claim: what your state believes should happen.
  • Mechanism: how the Council can make it happen.
  • Result: what changes for affected people or institutions.
  • Impact: why the change matters for dignity, justice, and protection.

Example

If your claim is that access to independent reporting improves protection, the mechanism is Special Procedures and monitoring, the result is better documentation and pressure for compliance, and the impact is stronger accountability and fewer unchecked violations.

Final takeaway

The UNHRC rewards delegates who are informed, balanced, and practical. The strongest position is the one that can survive scrutiny and still protect rights.

In real life, the Human Rights Council is a central UN forum for addressing violations, coordinating human rights protection, and reviewing states through established mechanisms. In MUN, it becomes a space where language, evidence, and diplomacy must work together to produce realistic solutions.
